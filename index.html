Tool workflow
Overview

This tool translates raw GC SimDist (simulated distillation) data into predictive models for two classes of separation equipment: a wiped film evaporator (WFE) and a fractional distillation column.

The tool has three tabs. SimDist — upload a raw differential GC signal, click to identify the two primary component peaks, and the tool extracts pseudo-binary boiling points, feed composition, and ΔH_vap values. These seed a Clausius-Clapeyron VLE model that predicts relative volatility across pressure and composition, shown in a design space heatmap. Real Units — apply the VLE model to real equipment digital twins: WFE units (Pope Scientific and VTA GmbH) and fractional distillation columns (bench batch through industrial continuous). Docs is this section.

Key outputs: distillate purity (y₁, wt% light component), bottoms composition (x₁), and light component yield (% of feed light component recovered in distillate). For WFEs these are shown as stat cards and contour heatmaps over the T_wall × flow rate operating space. For fractional columns they are shown on a McCabe-Thiele diagram with a reflux sweep chart.

VLE model and K-value flash
Component K-values

Each component's K-value is computed from its saturation pressure relative to the total operating pressure, evaluated at the bubble point temperature:

K_i(T_bubble, P) = P_sat,i(T_bubble) / P

K₁ > 1 means the light component preferentially partitions to vapor. K₂ < 1 means the heavy component prefers liquid. The relative volatility α = K₁/K₂ is not a constant — it varies with both temperature and pressure because the two components have different ΔH_vap values (different dHR in the Clausius-Clapeyron model), so their saturation pressure curves diverge at different rates. Lower pressure → lower T_bubble → the two Psat curves are further apart → higher α. This is why lower operating pressure always improves separation for this class of system.

Constrained flash at specified V/F (WFE)

For the WFE, V/F is set externally by the heat balance. Compositions follow from the material balance and K-value equilibrium, then normalised:

x_i = z_i / (1 + (V/F)·(K_i − 1))   → normalise to Σx_i = 1
y_i = K_i · x_i   → normalise to Σy_i = 1
Assumption: K-values are evaluated at the bulk liquid bubble point temperature. The actual film surface temperature lies between T_bubble and T_wall, but T_bubble is a conservative and physically consistent choice since the film is close to saturation during evaporation.
SimDist pathway — Clausius-Clapeyron VLE model
Differential signal upload and peak picking (Step 1 → Step 2)

The tool accepts a raw GC differential signal: a CSV with columns T_C (atmospheric boiling point in °C) and signal (detector response, any units). This is the standard output format of ASTM D2887 / D7169 SimDist. Files with thousands of rows are handled without issue.

After upload, the differential signal is displayed on an interactive canvas. The user clicks the apex of each primary component peak — light component first, then heavy. The tool snaps to the nearest local maximum within ±20°C of each click to eliminate noise sensitivity. From the two identified peaks:

  • T_b1, T_b2 — extracted directly from the peak temperature coordinates
  • z₁ — estimated from the integrated signal area on each side of the valley between the two peaks
  • σ — estimated from the half-width at half-maximum of each peak (FWHM/2.355), averaged between the two components

The differential signal is then integrated numerically (trapezoidal rule) to produce the cumulative boiling point distribution. A two-component cumulative Gaussian model is overlaid for visual validation:

W(T) = 100·z₁·Φ((T − T_b1)/σ) + 100·(1−z₁)·Φ((T − T_b2)/σ)

T_b1, T_b2, z₁, and σ can be fine-tuned with number inputs and sliders after peak picking.

Assumption: The feed behaves as a pseudo-binary with two distinct boiling fractions, each with a roughly symmetric boiling point distribution. Real feeds often have skewed or multi-modal SimDist curves; the user should verify that the two picked peaks represent the dominant fractions and that the cumulative overlay matches the integrated data reasonably well before proceeding.
Clausius-Clapeyron saturation pressure (Step 3)

Each pseudo-component's saturation pressure is computed from the Clausius-Clapeyron equation anchored at the atmospheric normal boiling point:

ln P_sat,i(T) = (ΔH_vap,i / R) · (1/T_b,i − 1/T_K) + ln(P_atm)

where ΔH_vap,i/R (units: K) is estimated from the ΔH_vap/R power-law correlation below. Evaluating at T_bubble and dividing by P gives K_i. Because dHR₁ ≠ dHR₂, both K-values and their ratio α vary with pressure through T_bubble.

Bubble point calculation

T_bubble at a given pressure P and composition z₁ is found by Newton-Raphson iteration of:

z₁·P_sat,1(T) + (1−z₁)·P_sat,2(T) = P

Converges to within 0.002°C in typically 10–20 iterations. T_bubble responds correctly to changes in P (lower P → lower T_bubble) and z₁ (more heavy component → higher T_bubble). The bubble point is re-solved at every operating point in both the WFE and fractional distillation models.

Design space and α heatmap (Step 4)

Step 4 shows the VLE-predicted design space: stream compositions and yields vs pressure and D/F split, T-xy and x-y equilibrium diagrams, and a heatmap of relative volatility α across the pressure × feed composition space.

The α heatmap axes are operating pressure (x, 0.1–15 Torr) and feed light fraction z₁ (y, 5–95 wt%). At each grid point, T_bubble is solved by Newton-Raphson for that specific (P, z₁) combination, and α = K₁/K₂ = P_sat,1(T_bubble)/P_sat,2(T_bubble) is evaluated at that temperature. This correctly captures the joint T-P dependence: lower P → lower T_bubble → larger α, because dHR₁ ≠ dHR₂.

VLE dataset save and load

Clicking Save VLE dataset in Step 3 or Step 4 downloads a .vle.json file containing: T_b1, T_b2, z₁, σ, dHR₁, dHR₂, the fitted α regression coefficients, and MW1/MW2. In the Real Units tab, Load .vle.json restores the full VLE model. MW1 and MW2 are required by the Real Units heat balance to compute Cp and ΔH_vap via the n-alkane correlations — without them the heat balance cannot be evaluated correctly.

Physical property correlations
ΔH_vap/R power law (VLE model)

When molecular weights are provided, ΔH_vap/R for each pseudo-component is estimated from its normal boiling point and MW using a power law fitted to n-alkanes C10–C24 from NIST:

ΔH_vap / R = 164.823 · T_b^(−0.106) · MW^(0.833)   [T_b in K, result in K, errors < 0.5%]

This controls how strongly α responds to pressure changes. A larger difference between dHR₁ and dHR₂ means a steeper α-vs-P curve — lower pressure provides more benefit. If MW values are not supplied, both components use the same average dHR and α becomes pressure-independent.

Liquid Cp and ΔH_vap for the heat balance (Real Units — WFE)

The WFE energy balance requires Cp (liquid heat capacity) and ΔH_vap (enthalpy of vaporisation) for each component. These are computed from MW using separate linear correlations fitted to NIST n-alkane data (C10–C30):

C_p,liq = 2.054 + 0.000858 · MW   [kJ/kg·K, errors < 1.5% vs NIST C10–C30]
ΔH_vap = 315.2 − 0.3636 · MW    [kJ/kg at T_b, errors < 5% vs NIST C10–C30]

Mixture values: Cp_mix = z₁·Cp₁ + (1−z₁)·Cp₂, similarly for ΔH_vap,mix, both evaluated at the current feed composition z₁. These are re-evaluated at every operating point in the WFE simulation.

Assumption: Both correlations assume n-alkane-like behaviour. Aromatic or polar fractions deviate — aromatics typically have higher ΔH_vap for a given MW. For non-paraffinic feeds, adjust MW inputs to match observed bubble points and heat balance behaviour.
Real Units — WFE model
WFE digital twins

Pre-configured units: Pope 2″ glass (electric, oil), Pope 4″ SS (electric, oil), Pope 6″ SS (oil, steam), Pope 12″ SS (oil, steam), VTA 6″ SS oil, and a custom user-defined entry. Each specifies heat transfer area A (m²), overall heat transfer coefficient U (W/m²·K), rated flow range (kg/hr), and maximum rated wall temperature. U values reflect heating medium: glass+electric ≈ 550, glass+oil ≈ 700, SS+electric ≈ 900, SS+oil ≈ 1300–1500, SS+steam ≈ 1650–1800 W/m²·K.

Energy balance — V/F from heat supply

The evaporation fraction V/F is determined by how much heat is available after warming any subcooled feed:

q = 1 + C_p · (T_bubble − T_feed) / ΔH_vap   [feed condition, shown in stat cards]
Q_avail = U · A · (T_wall − T_bubble)   [W]
Q_sensible = ṁ · C_p · (T_bubble − T_feed)   [W, only if T_feed < T_bubble]
Q_evap = max(0, Q_avail − Q_sensible)   [W]
V/F = min(0.95, Q_evap / (ṁ · ΔH_vap))   [dimensionless]

Higher T_wall → larger Q_avail → higher V/F. Higher ṁ → larger ṁ·ΔH_vap → lower V/F. Lower P → lower T_bubble → larger (T_wall − T_bubble) → higher Q_avail. All four operating inputs are physically connected to V/F and therefore to purity and yield.

Flow efficiency sigmoid

At high flow rates the wiped film thickens and residence time shortens, so vapor does not fully equilibrate with the liquid. The vapor composition is degraded between K-value equilibrium and the feed composition:

η(ṁ) = 1 / (1 + exp(a · (ṁ − 0.5·ṁ_max)))   [a = user-defined Flow correction factor, default 6]
y₁_actual = η · y₁_equil + (1 − η) · z₁
x₁_actual = (z₁ − (V/F) · y₁_actual) / (1 − V/F)   [mass balance]

The tipping point is 0.5·ṁ_max: below this η ≈ 1 (near-ideal); above it efficiency degrades rapidly. At ṁ_max, η ≈ 0.002. The steepness parameter a is adjustable in the operating conditions panel — lower values spread the transition over a wider flow range, higher values make the drop-off more abrupt. V/F is unaffected by η; only the vapor composition quality degrades.

Small units are thermally oversupplied: For the Pope 2″ glass at 0.1–1 kg/hr, U·A = 18 W/K means Q_evap/(ṁ·ΔH_vap) ≫ 0.95 at most wall temperatures. V/F is pinned at the 0.95 cap, making purity and yield insensitive to T_wall except near the bubble point or at the top of the rated flow range. This is a real physical property of the unit, not a model artifact.
Sigmoid not calibrated: The default a = 6 and 0.5·ṁ_max tipping point are physically motivated estimates, not measured values. Treat purity predictions above 0.5·ṁ_max as qualitative trend indicators until validated against real WFE distillate samples.
Real Units — fractional distillation model
McCabe-Thiele framework

All staged distillation units — batch or continuous — are modelled using the McCabe-Thiele graphical method applied to the binary VLE from the SimDist model. The equilibrium curve y = K₁·x/(K₁·x + K₂·(1−x)) is computed from K-values at the operating bubble point. All elements of the model (equilibrium curve, operating lines, staircase) are re-evaluated when any input changes.

Continuous columns (Oldershaw, sieve tray, custom continuous)

For continuous columns with a mid-column feed, q = 1 (saturated liquid feed at bubble point) is assumed. Three lines are drawn on the McCabe-Thiele diagram:

  • Rectifying operating line: y = (R/(R+1))·x + xD/(R+1), slope R/(R+1), connecting xD on the diagonal to the feed pinch at x = xF
  • q-line: vertical at x = xF (for q=1), connecting the diagonal to the rectifying line; marks the feed stage location
  • Stripping operating line: passes through (xB, xB) and the pinch point (xF, y_pinch) where y_pinch = (R/(R+1))·xF + xD/(R+1)

The staircase steps from xB upward using the stripping line until x crosses xF, then switches to the rectifying line for the remaining stages. xB is found by binary search: given N_eff stages, R, xF, and D/F, find xB such that the staircase exactly uses up all N_eff stages to reach xD = (xF − (1−D/F)·xB) / (D/F).

Batch columns (Vigreux, packed)

Batch columns have no mid-column feed — the still pot is the reboiler and the column above it is entirely a rectifying section. There is no feed stage, no stripping section, and no q-line. The correct model:

  1. Given a still-pot composition xB, find the instantaneous overhead composition xD by forward-solving: binary-search xD such that N_eff steps of (vertical to VLE curve, horizontal to rectifying line) starting from xB reach xD
  2. Binary-search xB to satisfy the overall charge balance: xF = D/F · xD + (1−D/F) · xB

This correctly treats xB as the still-pot composition at the moment of snapshot, and xD as the composition the column delivers at that instant. The mass balance relates xB and xD through D/F and xF.

Batch simplification: The model is a steady-state snapshot at one pot composition. Real batch distillations have continuously evolving still-pot composition — xD changes throughout the run as the pot is depleted. This snapshot represents the instantaneous separation performance when the pot composition is xB.
Effective stages and Murphree efficiency

N_actual = round(N_theoretical × Murphree efficiency). The Murphree efficiency accounts for imperfect vapor-liquid contact per stage — real plates or packing do not achieve full thermodynamic equilibrium. Typical values: Vigreux 50–60%, packed 60–70%, sieve tray 65–75%. The effective stage count is fixed by the selected unit and cannot be exceeded by any operating condition.

Reflux sweep chart

Shows distillate purity xD across the full reflux range R = 0.5–20 at fixed D/F, xF, and P. For batch columns the sweep uses the corrected forward solver at each R. For continuous columns it uses the full rectifying + stripping binary search. The current R is marked with a red dot. The leftmost point where purity begins to rise marks the practical minimum reflux for this column and separation; the curve flattening at high R indicates diminishing returns above roughly 1.5× R_min.

Key limitations
Binary pseudo-component only
All equations assume a two-component system. Multi-modal or broad SimDist curves are poorly approximated by two Gaussians. Separation quality degrades as boiling ranges widen or the two fractions overlap heavily. Sub-peaks between the two main components are ignored after peak-picking.
Ideal VLE assumed
Raoult's law (ideal liquid phase, ideal gas phase) is assumed throughout. Non-ideal mixtures — strongly polar, hydrogen-bonding, or wide polarity mismatch systems — require NRTL, UNIQUAC, or activity coefficient models not implemented here.
WFE flow efficiency not calibrated
The sigmoid efficiency model (default a = 6, tipping point at 0.5·ṁ_max) is physically motivated but not measured. Treat purity predictions above 0.5·ṁ_max as qualitative trends until validated against real WFE distillate samples at multiple flow rates.
n-Alkane property correlations
Cp, ΔH_vap, and ΔH_vap/R are estimated from MW via correlations fitted to n-alkanes C10–C30. Aromatic, naphthenic, or polar fractions will deviate. For components with MW outside ~140–340 g/mol, treat bubble points and heat balance outputs as approximate.
U values are estimates
Overall heat transfer coefficients are derived from published performance data and engineering estimates. Actual U depends on feed viscosity, wiper speed, film thickness, and fouling. High-viscosity feeds reduce the product-side film coefficient, lowering effective Q_avail below the model prediction.
Fractional distillation — snapshot only
The batch distillation model shows a steady-state snapshot at one still-pot composition, not a time-resolved simulation of pot depletion. Continuous column models assume q=1 (saturated liquid feed) and optimal feed stage placement. Non-ideal plate behaviour beyond Murphree efficiency (e.g. entrainment, weeping) is not modelled.
Real unit operations
From ideal design space to real equipment
The design space tab predicts separation at full thermodynamic equilibrium. Real equipment departs from this ideal based on heat transfer area, heating duty, feed temperature, and throughput. This section layers those constraints on top of the VLE model.
How the model works
Each operating point is computed from an energy balance and a constrained K-value flash:
  • Bubble point: computed from P and z₁ using Clausius-Clapeyron. K-values K₁ = P_sat,1(T_bubble)/P and K₂ = P_sat,2(T_bubble)/P follow directly.
  • Sensible heating: if T_feed < T_bubble, some Q_avail is consumed warming the feed. The q-factor = 1 + Cp·(T_bubble−T_feed)/ΔH_vap quantifies this penalty.
  • V/F from heat balance: V/F = Q_evap / (ṁ · ΔH_vap), capped at 0.95. Higher T_wall → more Q_avail → higher V/F. Higher ṁ → larger denominator → lower V/F.
  • Compositions from constrained flash: x_i = z_i/(1+(V/F)·(K_i−1)), normalise, y_i = K_i·x_i, normalise. K-values evaluated at T_bubble.
  • Flow efficiency sigmoid: η = 1/(1+exp(6·(ṁ−0.5·ṁ_max))) degrades vapor composition toward feed at high throughput. y₁_actual = η·y₁_equil + (1−η)·z₁. V/F is unaffected — only composition quality degrades.
C_p and ΔH_vap come from n-alkane MW correlations. See the Docs tab for full equations and assumptions.

Fractional distillation digital twins
What this section models and how to use it

A fractional distillation column achieves separation through repeated vapor-liquid equilibrium stages. Each stage enriches the vapor in the light component; the enriched vapor rises to the next stage and is further purified. This is fundamentally different from a WFE, which is a single equilibrium stage — the column can approach nearly pure components given enough stages and sufficient reflux, at the cost of time and energy.

The VLE constraint: the equilibrium curve drawn on the McCabe-Thiele diagram is dictated entirely by the relative volatility α from your SimDist model. A higher α (larger boiling point gap, lower pressure) bows the curve further from the diagonal, and fewer stages are needed to cross from feed composition to distillate spec. You cannot exceed the equilibrium curve — no amount of reflux or stages allows you to extract a vapor richer than the equilibrium vapor above a given liquid. This curve is the fundamental physical limit of the separation.

The equipment constraint: the number of effective stages N_actual = N_theoretical × Murphree efficiency is fixed by the column you select. No operating condition can exceed it. If the staircase on the McCabe-Thiele diagram requires more steps than N_actual to reach the desired distillate purity, you will not achieve that purity with this column — you need more stages, higher reflux (which steepens the operating line and shortens steps), lower pressure (which increases α), or a wider D/F cut (which relaxes the purity requirement).

Unit specifications
Custom unit parameters
Operating conditions
R = L/D: liquid returned to column per unit distillate collected. Higher R steepens the operating line on the McCabe-Thiele diagram, shortening each step and allowing higher distillate purity for a fixed number of stages. The tradeoff is reduced throughput — more liquid is recycled and less is collected as product. There is a minimum reflux ratio R_min below which no finite number of stages can achieve the desired separation; the operating line touches the equilibrium curve (pinch point). Above R_min, increasing R always improves purity but with diminishing returns. Total reflux (R → ∞) gives maximum purity but zero throughput.
Operating pressure sets the column temperature via the bubble point and — critically — the relative volatility α. Lower pressure → lower T_bubble → higher α because the two components' vapor pressures diverge more at lower temperature (they have different ΔH_vap values). Higher α bows the equilibrium curve further from the diagonal, requiring fewer stages or lower reflux to achieve the same purity. Vacuum operation also reduces thermal degradation of heat-sensitive materials. Watch the equilibrium curve on the McCabe-Thiele diagram shift as you change pressure.
The weight fraction of the light component entering the column. This sets the feed point on the McCabe-Thiele diagram and, together with D/F, determines the mass balance. Richer feeds (higher x_F) make the separation easier — the feed point is closer to the distillate spec and fewer stages are needed. Leaner feeds push the staircase further to the left, requiring more stages to climb to the distillate composition. x_F also controls how much light component is available to recover — a lean feed places an absolute ceiling on distillate yield regardless of column performance.
The fraction of feed collected as distillate (D/F = D/(D+B)). This is the primary purity-yield lever: a smaller D/F cut produces purer distillate because a narrower slice of the top of the composition profile is collected, but recovers less of the light component. A larger D/F sacrifices purity to recover more product. The mass balance constraint xD·D + xB·B = xF·F is always satisfied — reducing D/F raises xD but lowers yield, and vice versa. The operating line intercept xD/(R+1) on the y-axis shifts as D/F changes, rotating the operating line and reshaping the staircase.

How to read the McCabe-Thiele diagram
The equilibrium curve (purple) shows the vapor composition y in equilibrium with liquid x at operating pressure. Its shape is entirely determined by α from the VLE model — it cannot be changed by equipment or operating choices, only by pressure (which changes α) or by using a different feed. The further it bows above the diagonal, the easier the separation.

Operating lines: the blue rectifying line covers the column above the feed (slope R/(R+1), intercept xD/(R+1)). For continuous columns, the green stripping line covers the section below the feed (steeper slope, passes through xB). The orange q-line is vertical at xF for a saturated liquid feed (q=1) and marks where the two operating lines intersect. For batch columns there is no feed stage — only the rectifying line applies.

The staircase (red) represents the actual stages. Each step rises vertically from the operating line to the equilibrium curve (one stage of vapor-liquid contact), then steps horizontally back down to the operating line. For continuous columns, the staircase switches from the stripping line to the rectifying line at the feed stage. Count the steps to verify how many theoretical stages the separation requires. If the staircase cannot reach xD in N_actual steps, the column is undersized — increase N, raise R, or lower pressure to increase α.

xD, xF, xB are marked as coloured dots on the diagonal with dashed vertical guides. The staircase begins at xB (bottoms) and terminates at xD (distillate). If xD is very close to 100% and xB very low, the steps may be visually compressed into the corners — this indicates the separation is very easy (large α) and the column is significantly over-designed for this duty. The reflux sweep chart on the right shows how distillate purity varies across the full reflux range; the red dot marks your current R.
